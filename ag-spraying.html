Why Farmers Choose Drone Spraying

The practical advantages over traditional methods

Spray When Fields Are Wet

No ground compaction. Get product down right after rain when ground equipment would make ruts.

Zero Crop Damage

No wheel tracks through your field. Spray tall corn and beans without knocking down a single plant.

Fast Turnaround

Most pilots can be on-site within days, not weeks. No waiting in line behind big operations.

GPS Precision

Every pass logged. No overlaps, no misses. Variable rate application where supported.

No Minimums on Small Fields

That awkward 40 acres across the road? Drones handle small and irregular fields cost-effectively.

Access Tough Terrain

Hillsides, waterways, around obstacles. Drones go where ground rigs and planes can't.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions from farmers about drone spraying

Drone crop spraying typically costs $8-18 per acre depending on the application type, field size, and your location. Cover crop seeding runs $10-15/acre, fungicide/herbicide application $12-18/acre, and large acreage jobs often qualify for volume discounts. Most operators have minimum charges of $200-500.
Agricultural drones can apply cover crop seed, fungicides, herbicides, pesticides, liquid fertilizer, and specialty treatments. They're particularly effective for cover crop seeding into standing corn/soybeans, late-season fungicide when ground equipment would damage crops, and spot-treating problem areas.
Modern ag drones like the DJI T50 can spray 50-80 acres per hour under ideal conditions, meaning 400-600+ acres per day is achievable. Actual output depends on field shape, obstacles, refill logistics, and weather. Most operators can complete 200-400 acres in a full working day.
Yes, commercial drone operators must hold an FAA Part 107 license. For pesticide/herbicide application, many states also require a pesticide applicator license. Always verify your pilot has proper FAA certification and any required state agricultural licenses before hiring.
Drones excel when: fields are too wet for ground equipment, crops are too tall to drive through without damage, you need precise spot-treatment, you're seeding cover crops into standing crops, dealing with irregular field shapes or obstacles, or need quick turnaround after rain. They're also ideal for small/medium fields where mobilizing ground equipment isn't cost-effective.
Drones are cost-competitive for fields from 20-500 acres. Under 20 acres, minimum charges may apply. Over 500 acres, traditional aerial application might be more economical, though drones still win on precision and flexibility. Many farmers use drones for smaller fields and problem areas while using planes for large open fields.
